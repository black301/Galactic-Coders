Mercury

Mercury is the smallest planet in our Solar System and the closest to the Sun. Its surface is heavily cratered, similar to Earth's Moon.

Despite being the closest planet to the Sun, Mercury is not the hottest planet - that title belongs to Venus. Mercury's temperatures swing wildly between extreme heat and cold.

A year on Mercury (one orbit around the Sun) takes just 88 Earth days, but a single Mercury day lasts about 176 Earth days.

Venus

Venus is often called Earth's twin due to their similar size, but it's a hostile world with a thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide and sulfuric acid clouds.

It's the hottest planet in our Solar System, with surface temperatures hot enough to melt lead. This is due to an extreme greenhouse effect.

Venus rotates backwards compared to most other planets, and its day is longer than its year!

Earth

Earth is our home planet and the only known world that supports life. It's the third planet from the Sun and the largest of the rocky planets.

Our planet's unique atmosphere, liquid water, and magnetic field make it perfectly suited for life as we know it.

Earth is accompanied by one natural satellite, the Moon, which influences our tides and helps stabilize our climate.

Mars

Mars is known as the Red Planet due to iron oxide (rust) on its surface. It's home to both the highest mountain and the deepest canyon in the Solar System.

Scientists have found evidence of ancient rivers and lakes on Mars, suggesting it once had liquid water on its surface.

Mars has two small moons, Phobos and Deimos, and is a prime target for future human exploration.

Jupiter

Jupiter is the largest planet in our Solar System and the first of the gas giants. Its Great Red Spot is a giant storm that has been raging for hundreds of years.

With at least 79 moons, Jupiter has the most satellites of any planet in our Solar System. The four largest, called the Galilean moons, are Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.

Jupiter's powerful magnetic field is the strongest of all the planets, creating intense radiation belts.

Saturn

Saturn is famous for its spectacular ring system, composed mainly of ice particles, rocky debris, and dust. Despite their vast size, the rings are incredibly thin.

Like Jupiter, Saturn is a gas giant, primarily composed of hydrogen and helium. It's the only planet less dense than water - it would float in a giant bathtub!

Saturn has 82 confirmed moons, with Titan being the largest and most intriguing due to its thick atmosphere and liquid methane lakes.

Uranus

Uranus is an ice giant planet that uniquely rotates on its side, likely due to a massive impact early in its history. This gives it extreme seasons lasting decades.

The planet's blue-green color comes from methane in its atmosphere, which absorbs red light and reflects blue-green light.

Uranus has 27 known moons, all named after characters from the works of William Shakespeare and Alexander Pope.

Neptune

Neptune is the windiest planet in our Solar System, with speeds reaching 1,200 mph. Its dark spots are giant storms similar to Jupiter's Great Red Spot.

Like Uranus, Neptune is an ice giant, composed mainly of water, ammonia, and methane ices beneath its atmosphere.

Neptune has 14 known moons, with Triton being the largest. Triton orbits backwards compared to Neptune's rotation and is likely a captured dwarf planet.
